# Projet Halima

Projet Halima carries the name of our mother, Halima, and carries forward two missions in her memory.

The first: books should not arrive in a community like visitors. They should become part of the community's memory, discipline, and future. Since 2019, we build school libraries in Niger and train students to become **Guardians of Knowledge**: young custodians who learn how to protect, organize, share, and sustain the books entrusted to their school.

The second: Halima passed away from diabetes, so each year our family stands with 20 to 30 people living with diabetes in Niger — in her memory, and in their corner.

The work began in 2019 in Maradi, Niger, and is named after my mother, Halima.

## Why I Am Doing This

My mother taught English in Maradi. But the deeper lesson she left me was not only about language. It was about respect for knowledge.

Growing up, books were not treated as disposable objects in our home. They were protected. You did not tear a page, bend a spine, or leave a book carelessly on the floor. A book represented effort: someone wrote it, someone printed it, someone carried it, someone paid for it, and someone might need it after you.

My father ran a bookstore in Maradi, so I also grew up watching people come in for books, school supplies, typed documents, and printed pages. The bookstore taught me that access to information is practical. It is not an abstract ideal. It changes what a student can study, what a parent can submit, what a school can keep track of, and what a young person can imagine for themselves.

After my mother passed away, English became one of the ways I stayed connected to her. An American family helped me learn the language, first through weekly visits and later through long summer sessions. English opened doors that took me from Maradi to university in the United States, then to a career building software systems at Bloomberg and Google.

That experience shaped a simple conviction: opportunity often begins with access, but access only lasts when people know how to protect and use what they have.

Projet Halima is my attempt to carry that conviction home.

## The Problem

Many education projects fail after the donation ceremony.

A library is built. Books are delivered. Photos are taken. Everyone means well. But two years later, the shelves are broken, the books are torn or missing, and the room has become storage.

The problem is not only lack of resources. It is lack of stewardship.

If students are not taught that the books belong to them, they will treat the library as something external. If teachers and parents are not involved, the library becomes a room instead of a habit. If the village does not feel ownership, the project depends forever on the person who donated it.

Infrastructure fades. Culture stays.

## What We Do

Projet Halima does not only donate books. We build the culture that keeps books alive.

Our work has four parts:

1. **Meet the community.** We begin with village chiefs, elders, teachers, parents, and school leaders. Trust comes before construction.
2. **Make reading meaningful.** We gather the community around the value of literacy before books arrive, so the library starts as a shared commitment instead of a shipment.
3. **Build and equip libraries.** We create library spaces and place books where students can use them.
4. **Train Guardians of Knowledge.** We train students in the protocols of care: how to handle books, organize them, protect them, lend them responsibly, and pass that discipline to others.

A donated book can disappear in a year. A protected book can serve a generation.

## Our Impact So Far

- 4 libraries built
- 1,000+ books stewarded
- 1,300+ students reached
- 50+ schools trained through Guardian-centered literacy work
- 20–30 people living with diabetes supported each year, in Halima's memory

These numbers matter, but the deeper measure is whether the libraries still matter after the excitement fades, and whether the families we support each year feel less alone. Projet Halima is designed for that long middle: the ordinary days when students open books, return them, repair them, recommend them, and learn that knowledge is something they are responsible for — and the ordinary years when our family keeps showing up for people fighting the disease that took Halima from us.

## The Philosophy

I build software for a living, so I often think in systems.

A book is not enough. A shelf is not enough. A library is not enough.

Those are the hardware.

The software is the culture around them: the routines, expectations, pride, and responsibility that make people care enough to maintain what was built.

Projet Halima is not charity in the simple sense of giving. It is stewardship. It asks a harder question:

> What would it take for this resource to still be useful ten years from now?

That question changes the work. It forces us to care about training, ownership, maintenance, and dignity. It makes the community the center of the project, not the recipient of it.

## Why Her Name

This project carries my mother's name because it carries her discipline.

Halima believed knowledge deserved respect. She taught English, but more than that, she taught that learning is not passive. You receive knowledge, you care for it, and you pass it forward.

Every library we build is a small continuation of that lesson.

Not a monument. A practice.

## Built For The Long Term

Projet Halima is part of a broader belief behind the work I do across education and technology: people in low-resource environments are not short on ambition, creativity, or discipline. They are often short on systems that respect their reality.

The goal is not to import fragile solutions that only work under perfect conditions. The goal is to build with the community, for the conditions that actually exist, and in a way that can endure.

Projet Halima is one expression of that belief:

**Build libraries. Train guardians. Sustain knowledge.**
